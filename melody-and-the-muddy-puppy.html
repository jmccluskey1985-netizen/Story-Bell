Melody and the Muddy Puppy

Melody loved her puppy Biscuit more than anything in the world. Biscuit was a golden ball of fluff with floppy ears and a waggy tail that never stopped moving. Every Saturday, Melody and Biscuit went to Sunshine Park to play.

"Come on, Biscuit!" Melody called, clipping on his blue leash. "Let's go have an adventure!"

Biscuit barked happily and bounced toward the door. Today was going to be a wonderful day.

The park was beautiful and sunny. Green grass sparkled with morning dew, and colorful flowers bloomed along the paths. Biscuit trotted beside Melody, his fluffy golden fur shining in the sunlight.

They played fetch with Biscuit's favorite tennis ball. They ran through the grass. They said hello to other dogs and their owners. Everything was perfect.

"You're such a good, clean puppy," Melody said, patting Biscuit's soft, fluffy head.

Biscuit licked her hand and wagged his tail.

But then, Biscuit's nose started twitching. He had smelled something interesting. His ears perked up, and he pulled at the leash, leading Melody toward the edge of the park.

There, behind a cluster of trees, was the biggest, muddiest, most gigantic puddle Melody had ever seen. Yesterday's rain had created a chocolatey-brown pool of thick, gooey mud.

"Oh no, Biscuit," Melody said, holding the leash tighter. "We're not going near that yucky puddle. You'll get all dirty!"

But Biscuit had other ideas.

Before Melody could stop him, Biscuit pulled free from his collar and—SPLASH!—jumped right into the middle of the muddy puddle!

Mud flew everywhere. It splattered on trees. It splattered on grass. It even splattered on Melody's shoes!

Biscuit rolled in the mud. He wiggled in the mud. He seemed to think this was the most wonderful thing that had ever happened to him.

"Biscuit, NO!" Melody cried.

When Biscuit finally climbed out of the puddle, Melody couldn't believe her eyes. Her beautiful, fluffy, golden puppy was gone. In his place stood a creature completely covered in thick, dark, sticky mud.

Melody's nose wrinkled up. Her eyebrows scrunched together. Her mouth turned down in a frown.

"Ewwww!" Melody said, taking a step back. "Biscuit, you're... you're disgusting!"

Biscuit wagged his tail happily, not understanding what the problem was. He smelled terrible—like wet dirt and old leaves. Bits of mud dripped off his ears.

Melody felt something she'd never felt so strongly before. Her tummy felt funny, like she might be sick. She didn't want to touch Biscuit at all. This feeling was disgust.

Biscuit trotted toward Melody, and then—as dogs do—he started to shake himself dry.

"No, no, NO!" Melody shouted, jumping backward.

But it was too late. Biscuit shook from head to tail, and muddy water sprayed everywhere like a brown fountain. Droplets of mud landed on Melody's jeans, her jacket, and even in her hair!

"YUCK!" Melody exclaimed, wiping mud off her cheek. Her disgust feeling got even bigger. She could feel her face scrunching up more and more.

Melody knew they had to get home. Carefully, holding her breath, she picked up Biscuit's leash and held it at arm's length—as far away from the muddy puppy as possible.

"Come on, you messy monster," Melody said, trying not to laugh even though she felt so disgusted. "We need to get you in the bath right now."

People they passed on the street stared at muddy Biscuit. Some laughed. Some said, "Oh my!" One little boy pointed and giggled.

Melody's cheeks turned pink with embarrassment, but she kept walking. Her clean, pretty puppy had turned into a mud monster, and she knew exactly what needed to happen next.

At home, Melody's mum took one look at Biscuit and burst out laughing. "Oh dear! Someone found a puddle!"

"It's not funny, Mum," Melody said, though she was starting to smile a tiny bit. "He's absolutely disgusting!"

"Well," Mum said, "disgust is actually a very useful feeling, Melody. It's our body's way of telling us that something might not be clean or safe. It helps protect us from germs and illness."

Together, they filled the bathtub with warm water and lots of bubbly soap. Biscuit didn't like baths very much, but he needed one now!

Melody rolled up her sleeves. "Okay, Biscuit. In you go!"

Splish, splash, splosh! Water went everywhere as Melody scrubbed and scrubbed. Mud swirled in the water, turning it brown. Biscuit wiggled and shook, getting Melody even wetter.

"You're making such a mess!" Melody laughed, wiping soap bubbles from her nose. The disgust feeling was going away now. This was actually kind of funny!

After three rinses and lots of shampooing, Biscuit was finally clean again. Melody wrapped him in a big, fluffy towel and dried him off. His golden fur appeared again, soft and clean and smelling like fresh flowers.

"There's my beautiful puppy!" Melody said, giving Biscuit a big hug. "You're not disgusting anymore!"

Biscuit licked her face happily, his tail wagging like crazy.

Melody's mum sat down next to them. "So, Melody, what did you learn about disgust today?"

Melody thought for a moment. "I learned that disgust is a feeling that happens when something is really yucky or dirty. It makes your nose wrinkle and your tummy feel funny."

"That's right," Mum said. "And what else?"

"That disgust helps us know when we need to clean something or stay away from something that might have germs," Melody continued. "And also... that even when something is really, really disgusting, it can be fixed. Biscuit was covered in mud, but after a bath, he's perfect again!"

"Very wise," Mum said proudly.

Melody giggled. "And I also learned that even though disgust feels really strong and uncomfortable, it can actually be a little bit funny too. I mean, you should have seen Biscuit's face when he was all muddy!"

That night, as Melody cuddled with clean, fluffy Biscuit on the sofa, she thought about her adventure. Yes, she had felt disgusted—very disgusted! But she had also learned that it was just a feeling, and feelings don't last forever. Messy things can be cleaned up. And sometimes, the yuckiest moments make the best stories.

Biscuit yawned and rested his head on Melody's lap, completely clean and smelling wonderful.

"Next time," Melody whispered, "we're staying far away from mud puddles. Deal?"

Biscuit's tail wagged once. But Melody had a feeling that next Saturday, if they saw another puddle, Biscuit might have other ideas.

And that was okay. Because now Melody knew—she could handle a little disgust, some mud, and a whole lot of puppy love.

**The End**
